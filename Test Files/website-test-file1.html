Jakarta’s Choco Market

Go for Chocolate,
Go International,
Go to Jakarta?

This is a fictive international marketing blog about ongoing investigations in Jakarta (Indonesia) on behalf of “OCHO Chocolate”. The aim is to explore and analyze the foreign market environment to assess Jakarta’s attractiveness for a market entry initiative.

OCHO is a young company founded by Liz Rowe in 2013. Crowdfunding made it possible to raise two million dollars in just two days. OCHO’s idea is it to find beauty in simplicity and sustainability. Fairtrade cacao beans from the pacific, community ownership and good working conditions characterize the company’s philosophy and culture. They believe that chocolate is something you can and should feel good about. The chocolate is exclusively produced in the OCHO Chocolate Factory in Dunedin.

Go to Jakarta?

After all investigation and first contacts with potential business partners, it is time for some final conclusions. The time has been nice, the weather was great and the food was delicious. But now it’s time to go away from the smog and breath some fresh New Zealand air again.

I want to start with some good things. Jakarta is the economic, cultural and political centre of Indonesia. It’s an extremely lively city with a lot of business activities. The potential market is huge with 10 million mostly young people. Tourism and the high population density is favourable for selling chocolate in local shops. Heavy use of social media allows us to engage effectively in online marketing. People in Jakarta speak English which is good for building and maintaining business relationships.

The country experiences strong economic growth with an increasing buying power of consumers. We found good exporting infrastructure and can benefit from a free trade agreement between NZ and Indonesia. We see a strongly growing market for chocolate that comes along with an increasing domestic demand. Imported chocolate is the customer’s choice when it comes to taste and quality.

Going there will help to gain strategic assets like international experience, foreign market knowledge and a network of international partners. Building up strategic alliances to export our chocolate is not capital extensive and connected to a relatively low risk. We face little pressure for spending more resources on adapting our product but would be recommended in the long rung to sustain success.

On the other hand, we also have to talk about the negative aspects. The average net income in Jakarta is about 470 NZD. Our high production cost plus exporting will make that chocolate to a luxury product. That narrows our target market and the pool of potential business partners.

The market entry is difficult. It is important to build and maintain relationships to official authorities and business partners at the same time. Cultural distance and the predominant business culture make it time-consuming and difficult to realize business opportunities. On top, we likely interact with an environment that engages in corruptive practices. Working in that market can pose unforeseeable difficulties. This is also reflected in the low ranking of the Ease of Doing Business 2020 Report by the World Bank.

Despite the growing market, the chocolate consumption is still relatively low. There is no cultural or traditional connection to chocolate itself. Furthermore, 80% of the market is controlled by two major competitors. That is even more significant since customers prefer local brands. Our only competitive advantage is our high quality and appealing business ethics. But we need to consider the low country of origin effect regarding chocolate. The effect of those advantages might be limited and only appreciated by an even smaller target group.

Taking those arguments into account, we need to think about if the market is mature enough at that point of time. Building up the business will take time and while the market prospects are good it will be unlikely to gain any short term profits. However, we need to invest resources in maintaining the foreign business activities. Our weak competitive situation reduces the chances of long term success.

That brings me to OCHO itself. The company has no or little international experience. Going to Jakarta might be useful to gain experience and market knowledge but is it the right environment to start with? It might be too challenging in the face of worthwhile alternatives. OCHO has also no international reputation and we face the challenge of not being taken seriously in Jakarta. How can we show that we have the resources and capabilities to maintain successful international business relationships?

And last but not least, COVID-19. International travel and business will be difficult for an unforeseeable time. We might not be able to show the needed physical presence. On top of that, we face a serious risk for a global recession. A foreign market entry initiative seems to be inappropriate considering the economic uncertainty. Will our business partners be able to stay in business? Should we rather prefer to save up as many resources as possible and prepare for a recession?

My overall recommendation is not to go to Jakarta at this point of time. The possible advantages, that are mostly the gain of strategic assets, do not outway the effort, needed resources and risks. I believe our competitive advantages are more significant in other markets. I know that more teams are engaged in international investigations and strongly advice to consider alternatives. However, it is not impossible to enter that market. Since Jakarta has an emerging market for chocolate I recommend to carefully observe the market development. It can be worthwhile to enter the market once it reaches maturity.

Pressure for Local Responsiveness

Let me recall the I-R framework we talked about. I predicted that we have to choose between an international or multinational strategy depending on our advantages and forces for local responsiveness. Our entry strategy goes along with an international approach. However, most multinational companies that are engaged in the food sector follow a multinational strategy.

This is because food differs significantly from country to country and is influenced by the predominant culture and other factors. I believe that chocolate is more of a universal product. Meaning we do not have strong forces for local adaption which doesn’t mean we can’t gain any advantages from adaption.

If we make no changes to our product we face the lowes cost as it doesn’t require any changes in the production line and marketing mix. Considering that our product is already expensive for Jakarta and that exporting costs will also take a cut of our profit margin we want to minimize our adaptive efforts.

First, the price. As we want to gain market share we need to keep the price as low as possible. With an appropriate cost allocation system, we can determine the minimum selling price to cover our cost. Reducing the package size will lower the price for each unit of chocolate to attract more customers.

Second, the design. Have a look at the brand designs of Mayora Indonesia and Petra Food (picture below). We can see that they use a lot of dark red and brown. While those are typical colours for chocolate, OCHO uses besides the brown paper a more colourful approach. I would recommend adjusting the design accordingly. Concerning the language, I would stay with English as a product differentiation since people in Jakarta can read and speak the language. I would even consider adding some Maori elements for the same reason.

Third, the taste. I figured that most chocolate products from competitors are waffles, crunchy stuff and other wild chocolate combinations. But they all have one thing in common, they are sweet. So we are good to go with a variety of different tastes but the dark bitter chocolate might not fit to the customer preferences. We could also think about to introduce milk chocolate for this market.

Fourth, the marketing. In that market, it is very important to focus on the communication of superior quality and to highlight any differentiations. That includes:

  • Produced in New Zealand
  • “Local” cocoa beans
  • High-quality production
  • Sustainable and fari trade

Online marketing adapted to local preferences will be crucial. While the chocolate is marketed in NZ as a high-quality product we want to market it in Jakarta as an exclusive luxury product to justify the price and to attract the target group of well-situated customers. Those attributes must be also seen on the package.

Last but not least the aluminium foil in the package for temperature regulation purposes as the climate in Jakarta is hot.

Our Entry Strategy

We already discussed some considerable entry modes in the post about OCHO’s perspective of going international. However, we need to go more in-depth to that regard. The entry mode represents the entry strategy of the company and is a fundamental decision. We will put all the information that we gathered in a context to evaluate different options and to identify the best alternative.

What are our objectives for the foreign market entry initiative in Jakarta?

First, we want to gain access to a broader market. That means to increase production and revenues by selling the chocolate internationally. Second, we want to gain strategic assets. Entering the international market will enhance the sustaining capabilities of our company through international experience and foreign market knowledge. Third, is the objective to increase the brand’s reputation and therefore the value of OCHO chocolate. A company that is international active will have a better visibility, is more attractive to investors and has a stronger bargaining position towards potential business partners.

What are the opportunities and constraints in the market of Jakarta?

We learned that:

  • There is an emerging market for chocolate with attractive prospects for growth.
  • The buying power of Jakarta’s population is trending upwards thanks to a growing middle class.
  • We have low trade and entry barriers as a result of the free trade agreement.
  • Imported chocolate is preferred by customers that empathize quality.
  • Heavy use of social media opens up online marketing opportunities.
  • The effect of cultural and geographic distance is limited.

However, we also know, that:

  • Businesses and authorities are likely to be engaged in corruptive practices.
  • Entering a market requires good relationships.
  • Building up business relationships can be difficult and is time-consuming.
  • The market is dominated by big local players.
  • The chocolate consumption is relatively low.
  • The average disposable income in Jakarta is way below New Zealand’s.

OCHO’s Weaknesses in that Market

  • The company has no or little international experience.
  • The country of origin effect is weak.
  • The brand has no international reputation.
  • The production in NZ is costly which makes the product expensive.
  • The company has internally no or little availability of exclusive international market intelligence.
  • A network with international partners is not in place.

OCHO’s Strengths in that Market

  • An international competitive advantage through low trade barriers.
  • The chocolate is of high quality with an appealing background.
  • The company has the resources for online marketing and various entry modes.

Strategy. The information is important as it determines our anticipated timing, speed, scale and eventually the entry mode. Considering the cost of our product and the consumer behaviour it might be worthwhile to wait until the market is more mature. That is especially true the more resource extensive the entry mode is.

Since we are an internationally inexperienced company we are not looking for a fast and aggressive expansion. In contrast, we want to keep it slow and learn how to work with the new environment. Also, the market conditions and opportunities do not justify such an approach.

That brings us to the scale. The lack of experience poses a high risk potential. A large scale is resource extensive. Our situation requires a small-scaled entry to establish a foothold as a basis for learning and development.

Choosing the Entry Mode. Having these criteria in mind we can talk about different entry modes. It is important to know that some entry modes reduce resource requirements while others reduce risk or maximize opportunity. Therefore, we need to align the choice of the entry mode with our objectives, the market and non-market environment and our strategy.

We can exclude most of the equity modes for obvious reasons. The required resources and commitment do not fit to our strategy and capabilities. However, one exemption might be considerable. A brownfield project is a wholly-owned subsidiary where we use existing infrastructure like an empty shop to establish our own chocolate business. Such an entry mode would give us full control over all operations, a maximum of learning potential, high visibility in the city and has potentially the highest profitability. But if we compare our objectives with the market conditions and our capabilities it seems to be unrealistic to accept the high entry cost, risk and challenges. The risk-return tradeoff is out of balance.

Exporting can be a worthwhile option. We could export directly to our customers with an online shop. However, high individual shipping costs will lead customers to substitute products. Through a representative office, it is possible to indirectly export the chocolate through supermarkets and delicatessen shops. I would consider that approach more of a hybrid between direct and indirect exporting. It would give us a physical presence, direct contact with business customers and allows us to respond to market conditions. It would also foster the gain of market knowledge and international experience. On the other hand we will face high entry and increasing fix costs. We also need to invest resources to establish a supply chain. Even though it will allow us to build up strong business relationships over a longer time period it might be beyond our intended scale.

Indirect exports through domestic intermediaries would be the easiest and cheapest way but do not comply with our objectives. We might be able to increase sales but using those intermediaries lowers the profitability even more and deprives us from gaining international experience and market knowledge. The market opportunity is not significant enough to gain a sufficient advantage.

Considering all facts and arguments my favourite entry mode is indirect exporting in the form of strategic alliances. That means to have close relationships to a few local shops with collaborative arrangements in place that consider different aspects of the business.

The idea is to share resources without forming a joint venture to avoid major commitments. We can directly export to our business partners who sell the chocolate for us. However, we will engage in marketing activities to help our partners to sell their products and to promote our brand.

Since those partners are local distributors without the potential of becoming direct competitors we can establish an ongoing exchange of skill, international experience, foreign market knowledge and valuable business insights. It also provides most of the advantages that are related to direct exporting but without high investments and commitments. It is technical indirect exporting but shares many characteristics of a direct distribution channel. More about direct and indirect distribution channels.

However, building such relationships will be time-consuming and require a physical presence on a regular basis. But entering the market in cooperation with a local company seems to be the best way as we might face unforeseeable problems when establishing a foreign business in Jakarta.

This entry strategy would provide us with the foothold on a small scale that we need to learn about the market environment without taking too much risk. If it doesn’t go well, we can terminate the relationships but we still gained valuable experience that we can apply in a different target market. If we see potential for development and strong opportunities we can consider an expansion on a larger scale.

Cultural Challenges on the Point

We have discussed many cultural challenges in earlier blog posts. Don’t worry I will not go through all of them again. In this post, I will point out the most important ones that are crucial for us to successfully build business relationships and market our chocolate in Jakarta.

Corruption. An environment in which booth official institutions and businesses engage in corruptive practices can cause unforeseeable problems. We might face difficulties in entering the market and conduct business because competitors have indirect power over the responsible authorities. Potential business partners and authorities might expect favours or financial compensation in return for access to business opportunities. On the other hand, it is dangerous to engage in such transactions as it could lead to serious conflicts with the law. It is inevitable to be sensitive and to observe local practices.

Patience, persistence and presence. Building up relationships with officials and business partners needs time and personal effort. We can not expect to show up and go with a signed contract. In NZ we might be able to send out a business proposal, have a meeting, crunch the numbers and negotiate a contract. However, in Jakarta, we will need to build up trust first by showing commitment over a longer period of time. We need to be patient in our interactions even though we only have limited time.

Social Interaction. New Zealanders can be loud and passionate. They are also well known for a “piquant” sense of humour. A person from NZ takes space in a room with a strong presence. We have to pay attention to the cultural rules of social interaction. The left hand is considered to be dirty, avoid to use it. Be polite and gentle in all situations. Do not express negative feelings and avoid conflicts. Behave humble and speak softly. Be aware that a yes can be a no. It is important to read between the lines. Many of those rules are in contrast to NZ culture and create a significant challenge as it might be difficult to reflect and control our own behaviour. A relief for New Zealanders will be punctuality. It’s not so important to Indonesians but arriving in time can not hurt.

Customer Preferences. Since chocolate is an emerging market we do not know much about customer preferences. There is just not much culture around chocolate in Jakarta. McKinsey states in an article that even though Indonesia is a significantly growing consumer market Indonesians prefer local brands. On the other hand, Switzerland Global Enterprise says that customers that are looking for quality prefer imported chocolate. Ours is of high quality and made in NZ but we are not well known for good chocolate. Should we go to Jakarta we want to make the chocolate as appealing as possible. That means to point out what it makes it different to local chocolate but also to make it relatable to our customers. OCHO’s cocoa beans are imported from the pacific region. Indonesia is one of the biggest supplier in the world. So there is a chance that the beans are actually from Indonesia. That can be a marketing tool to work with. With the right communication on the package and a design that got some inspiration from elements of local chocolate products, we might be able to create a valuable hybrid. But this is a discussion about adaption and will be subject later on.

Indonesia’s Culture and Our Business

In my studies of intercultural management, I learned that culture is one of the most important aspects to consider when doing business abroad. It determines the “right” behaviour in a social environment and influences how people interact with each other. Cultural differences arise from different sets of norms, values, attitudes and beliefs between social groups that are ultimately originated in the peoples’ fundamental assumptions. The social culture of a country is closely interrelated with the business or professional culture.

But why is it important to be aware of the cultural differences? On the organizational level we can say that companies from different countries have different organizational practices, conflict resolution strategies and different management styles. High cultural distance usually discourages international trade and FDI. That means that we prefer low commitment, small investments and face more complicated business activities. However, the greater the cultural difference is the greater are the benefits from accessing local market knowledge. This is also true in marketing terms where the degree of cultural distance is important as it determines to a great extent the adjustments to the international marketing mix that can differ significantly to the local one. That depends in return on the pressure for local adaption that we will discuss later in the blog.

Let’s start with some general information about Indonesian culture. I use a Wikipedia article for a broad overview and a first impression. Indonesia’s indigenous culture has been influenced for a long time by foreign civilizations. This is because the country is located at major ancient trading routes. As a result, cultural practices are heavily influenced by a diversity of religions including most major world religions. Even though most of the population are Muslim the culture itself is a complex cultural mixture. The western culture has a significant impact on Indonesia’s science, technology, modern entertainment and political systems. We can assume that this cultural mix is especially present in Jakarta.

The GLOBE study is a more recent and extensive development of Hofstede’s approach to determine cultural differences. I’m going to point out some important dissimiliarities between NZ and Indonesia. Those differentiations will help us to identify important implications for our chocolate business. The Country Practice Score reflects how things are while the Country Value Score shows how people belief how thinks should be.

It is interesting to see that both cultures are very similar in most dimensions. New Zealanders are slightly more performance-orientated but less future-orientated. Surprising is that fairness and kindness is more valued in Indonesia than in NZ. Also important is that Indonesians feel a lot of pride in country and family and are loyal. New Zealanders are not but they think they are which is crucial to be aware of. Power distance is about the same in practice but New Zealanders value a lower power distance more.

That tells us that we need to concentrate also on longterm goals while building business relationships. Booth cultures value equally long term orientation but in practice is NZ more short term orientated than Indonesia. We also need to be careful to consider the social environment and to respect everyone appropriately. Family and cultural identity are important in Indonesia. New Zealanders share the same values but practice shows the opposite behaviour. We need to be aware that some of our perspectives might be inappropriate in this cultural context. Being aware of those differences will help us to avoid misunderstandings and will improve our cultural understanding.

Some aspects regarding the business culture are important to mention. It is absolutely crucial to show commitment of visiting the market on a regular basis. Otherwise, agents and distributors will not take foreign firms seriously. Patience, persistence and presence are the key success factors in Indonesia including Jakarta. Building up a business relationship takes care and time and potential business partners need to be chosen wisely. According to the U.S. International Trade Administration, it is not easy to terminate a bad relationship. Also, considering the culture of corruption, it’s good to maintain a wide network of local relationships.

In the Indonesian Culture Profile provided by the HAAC Multicultural Advisory Service you can find useful insights about many aspects of Indonesian’s culture especially about social interactions:

Though shaking hands is normal with introductions never shake with the left hand.

Pointing with a forefinger is considered impolite.

Friendliness is valued in Indonesia. Indonesians are known to hide negative feelings and avoid confrontation, rarely raising their voices.

Indonesians may also place less emphasis on punctuality. Be patient, as it is considered disrespectful to show signs of annoyance.

To save face and avoid shame, Indonesians may not directly disagree or say “No.”

Indonesians appreciate people speaking softly. Loud people may be interpreted as aggressive

Indonesians can be indirect communicators. They may not always say what they mean.

Indonesians do not seem to have a traditional relationship to chocolate. It is more of a luxury product and the consumption has been relatively low in the past. However, as already discussed in an earlier blog post the growing middle class leads to an increase in demand for the sweet temptation. Chocolate gains in popularity which creates an emerging market with high business opportunities. According to Statista, the market for chocolate confectionery grew in the last year by 6.3% with an expected annual growth rate of 3.8% from 2020 to 2023. Current revenues in that market segment amount to 7,872 million USD. Several sources confirm increasing business activities and prospects of growth. That implies that chocolate is on the best way to find its way into the Indonesians’ culture and households.

Indonesia’s People and Economy

In my first blog post, we already discovered some key aspects of geography and climate, political structures, demographic and social aspects as well as prices and information about consumer behaviour. I recommend reading the first blog post for some background information, especially about demographics. In this blog post, we will create a relevant (socio)economic profile of Jakarta that will help us to evaluate the desirability of our expansion plan.

The International Trade Administration of the U.S. Department of Commerce delivers useful insights about Indonesia for companies that want to do business in that country. The Indonesia Commercial Guide is a rather extensive source of information that helps to assess the countries attractiveness for a market entry initiative.

Let’s start with a little overview about the general conditions. Indonesia’s GDP in 2017 amounted to a bit more than 1,400 billion NZD with an average annual growth rate of 5% in the last decade. New Zealand’s GDP in 2019 was around 300 billion NZD. The current president seeks to reduce trade barriers and to improve the public infrastructure. However, I expect the infrastructure in Jakarta to be quite sophisticated. Indonesia experiences a growing domestic demand but despite the announcement of policies to encourage FDA protectionist policies, corruption and weak law enforcement create serious hurdles for foreign businesses and economic development. Indonesia is a developing democracy which reduces risks arising from an unstable political environment and is located at one of the world’s major trade routes which decreases logistical barriers. 54% of Indonesia’s population use the internet however, it is reasonable to assume that this number his significantly higher in Jakarta. Besides, it is important to mention that most people are heavy users of social media like Facebook, Twitter and Instagram which is excellent for advertising purposes. However, BBC states in Indonesia’s media profile that “many journalists self-censor because of the threat from laws on blasphemy and online libel.”

The market in Indonesia can be challenging for businesses. Indonesia is ranked 73 out of 190 on the Ease of Doing Business 2020 report by the World Bank. The Market Guide Indonesia provided by the NZTE states that “Indonesia’s import regulations are often complex and opaque” and recommend a close business relationship with the local agent to ease the market entry for food and beverages. The main reason is that 88% of Indonesia’s population is Muslim with strict religious requirements for food. We can see on the first glance the obvious challenges. First, we do not only need to foster good relationships with businesses but also with official agencies. The level of corruption might be helpful or a hurdle. That implies that we need time and adequate expertise. Also, it increases the risk of doing business which affects the choice of our reasonable entry moods.

On the other side, half of the NZ exports to Indonesia are food and beverages (548 million NZD in 2016). Diary exports were valued at 383 million NZD in 2016. That is a good indicator for the demand of NZ products. On top of that, there are no official tariffs or import restrictions what so ever on chocolate under the AANZFTA. Exports of chocolate from NZ to Indonesia only require a certificate of origin. Foreign companies located in Indonesia have the same tax obligations as a local resident. If all that reflects reality regarding trade barriers is questionable at that point. For more details have a look at the NZ Foreign Affairs & Trade Tariff Finder and the Market Guide.

According to an article from Switzerland Global Enterprise is Indonesia’s consumption of chocolate growing by 10% annually since 2017. The growing middle class increases the domestic demand for chocolate. The price sensitivity decreases and quality and taste gain in importance. The market for chocolate is worth more than 750 million USD. Even though Indonesia is the third-largest producer of cocoa beans the industry relies still on imports worth almost 90 million USD. I’m not sure if those are the latest numbers but the implication is that we have a big available market for the scale of OCHO chocolate.

The chocolate market is dominated by two local companies Petra Food and Mayora Indonesia with 80% of the total market share. Well known international brands share the last 20%. The local competitors have an advantage through strong visibility and low prices. However, the Swiss Business Hub Indonesia from Switzerland Global Enterprise beliefs that consumers that are looking for taste and quality prefer imported chocolate. That tells us that we are confronted with strong competition. But I believe that consumers are rather unexperienced and that brand loyalty is not a big issue yet as chocolate is an emerging market in Indonesia. The main distribution channels seem to be hypermarkets, supermarkets and convenient stores.

Ocho would have an advantage through lower trade barriers than other international brands. But the fact that OCHO chocolate is so expensive is still an issue. It is more of a luxury product than most other chocolate brands on the market. It is questionable if the market is ripe enough. On the other hand, it might be considered to adjust and globally integrate the value chain of the chocolate to produce a cheaper alternative just for Jakarta’s market but this would be a project on a large scale and might be beyond OCHO’s philosophy. Selling the chocolate and luxury boutiques in Jakarta is still an attractive possibility to explore.

Travel and Budget Requirements in Jakarta

Based on Jakarta’s climate information the temperature and sun hours are consistent during the year and therefore not relevant for a travel decision. However, the rain is. We do not want to go over there when it’s raining dogs and cats. Turns out the driest months are July to September. So we will consider this time period as a suitable travel time. I’m already happy to announce that everyone speaks English in Jakarta. That means we will be most likely not confronted with language barriers. The downside is the extremely poor air quality and smog in the city.

The Single Entry Visitor Visa B211A fits to our intended activity and is valid for 60 days. It can be extended by 30 days for a total of four times. It requires evidence of financial liquidity which is 1500 USD and a booked return ticket. Applying for this visa comes with a fee of 50 USD. New Zealand does not fall under any visa exemptions. There is currently an entry restriction for individuals being in mainland China in the past 14 days. Click on the link for further details.

Now is the question of how long we want and need to stay and how many people are about to go. We want to explore the local infrastructure, the market conditions for chocolate, cultural attitudes and implications as well as possible entry modes. That requires us to get in contact with different distributors, local shops and specialists. Therefore, I would propose a stay with two people of at least two months to tap deeper into relevant issues. Two persons will also find it more comfortable staying in a foreign country and it helps to discuss and plan the necessary efforts.

Nomadlist is an organisation, mentioned by BBC, New Your Times and CNN, that provides all different kind of information about most places in the world. The data that can be found comes from a huge user base and is constantly updated. According to their data, the living cost is 2000 NZD per month per person. That refers to a short term trip up to three months in relatively cheap hotels with private rooms and three outside meals per day in the centre of the city. Meaning the cost of living will add up to around 12,000 NZD for our intended trip.

The flights are averaging around 3000 NZD for two persons return. I have travelled quite a bit and prefer skyscanner to find the best flights. An example for a booking from the 1st of July to the 30th of September could look like the following with a total cost of 3,150 NZD for two persons:

Since our chocolate is rather expensive our area of interest would be also rather pricy with a lot of high-class shopping opportunities. That might drive up our accommodation expenses but keeps us close to our clientele. Therefore, considering the price brackets for accommodation in Jakarta, I add an additional 25 NZD per night per person for accommodation. That covers the needed upgrade in the centre of the city. Have a look at booking.com for closer insights. Doing so adds another 4,500 NZD to our three-month trip budget requirements.

Over there we will try to get an overall impression of marketing relevant aspects to sell chocolate. That includes but is not limited to the economic situation, consumer and business behaviour, culture, distribution channels and potential business partners. I can imagine to seek contact to relevant forums and talk to shop owners to express interest in business. The entire trip should be seen as a research project and should be performed like that.

Considering all relevant costs with a miscellaneous budget of 50 NZD per day I calculate an anticipated expenditure of 24,100 NZD as a maximum for a three-month stay. In case there is clear evidence against an entry initiative during the stay it can be cancelled at any time to safe time and costs. Therefore, I recommend booking flex tickets return.

Benefits and Challenges of Internationalisation

The last blog post was concerned with OCHO’s intentions, possibilities and consequences of going abroad. But we haven’t talked about the specific practical implications, benefits and challenges for OCHO chocolate.

The Challenges

What are the challenges and risks that come with a foreign market entry initiative and why should we go to Jakarta and investigate?

Let’s talk about the resources. Any initiative needs time and money, booth valuable resources to a company. Our choice of possible entry modes is not capital extensive but requires time. Especially the formation of alliances is time-consuming. However, the potential is significant. An investigation in Jakarta will help to test the waters and make some first contacts. The implementation of an international division that takes care of the foreign activities will be resource extensive, however, can become an additional profit centre for the company.

Furthermore, it is important to figure out if our product will sell in that market or if we need to adapt and respond to the local market conditions. The pressure for local responsiveness is influenced by the culture, the market environment, infrastructure as well as by things like the climate. The best way to figure that out is to get an insight-out point of view.

Talking about alliances we face the challenge of cultural distance that can have a negative impact on our business. We need to develop cultural capabilities to ensure effective business relationships and marketing results. We might have to adapt our business practices to a certain extent to the local template or it works the other way around. That depends highly on our own culture and the predominant culture in Jakarta. An EMIC approach is time-consuming but would provide the most important cultural information we need and is a good argument for an on-site investigation.

Last but not least we need to consider the status and reputation of New Zealand. We are not famous for chocolate, but we might be well known for a unique nature and sustainability. If that is an advantage we can work with, how do businesspeople in Jakarta think about business with New Zealand? Would they appreciate business opportunities, or would they hesitate? We also need to take logistical aspects into account as exports increase the cost of the chocolate.

The Benefits

The most obvious benefit is to increase sales and revenues. But this is just the tipping point. Selling a product abroad means to enter a foreign market which improves the economic strength and significance of OCHO chocolate. That is today more important than ever as the globalization fuels competition on an international level. 

Going abroad will also provide the company with valuable strategic assets. OCHO will learn to do business abroad, adapt to a different culture and will develop the company’s strategy, goals and values. That is a worthwhile contribution to OCHO’s sustaining capabilities.

Further, if we assume that the OCHO chocolate factory does not run on practicable capacity yet, it will reduce the production cost per unit and increase the profit margin in the short run. Also, we can offer the chocolate cheaper in Jakarta as long as we sell for more than the variable cost. In the long run, we might accomplish cost advantages through economies of scale.

If that is not enough, we can talk about the potential business alliances and their benefits. From a valuable business network to new business opportunities and tailored international marketing in cooperation with foreign partners. 

And last but not least; product life cycles. Wouldn’t it be great to have market alternatives in case that New Zealanders decide they have eaten enough OCHO chocolate? 

The list of potential benefits is extensive. If Jakarta turns out to be an attractive destination it is worthwhile to take the chance as the benefits overweigh the potential townsites. The resources needed to explore a potential market opportunity from within a country are neglectable compared to the potential benefits. The anticipated expenditure will be subject of the next blog post.

Going International – The OCHO Perspective

Prof. Dr Florian Becker-Ritterspach a professor of HTW Berlin told us what he believes is the mother of all questions in international business: 

Where do you locate?
What type of activity?
In which way?
What effect does this have on the firm and the environment?

Each question refers to an important dimension that needs to be considered when the business is being taken abroad. In this blog post, we will build the framework around OCHO’s foreign market entry initiative.

First, the location. Countries and even domestic regions differ from each other. We need to think about political, cultural, economic, legal and infrastructural differences and how they impact the way we do business. We will explore those aspects of Jakarta and the implications in future blog posts. 

Second, what is our intention? Do we want to seek for new markets, or do we go abroad to reduce certain costs along the value chain of our product? Perhaps we have a strategic motive and want to gain important assets for the company including knowledge about foreign markets, businesses, cultures and new technologies. Our location and intention determine significantly our way we will go abroad. I assume that OCHO’s intentions to go abroad are to increase sales through foreign markets and to gain international experience.

Third; the entry mode. We can choose between equity modes and non-equity modes. Each mode differs in the degree of control, systemic risk, dissemination risk and resource commitment. Since OCHO is a young, medium-sized and internationally inexperienced company we would prefer fast results with a low risk. That leads us to different options. 
Indirect export to export companies in NZ or wholesalers in Jakarta would be the easiest way to sell chocolate. However, that eliminates also any control in terms of prices and marketing or the gain of experience for the company. 
Direct export for example through an online shop might be more attractive to that regard. It gives OCHO more control but the access to the market might be limited.
A worthwhile possibility for OCHO could be to build strategic alliances with local chocolateries and delicatessen shops like “Better Chocolate Than Never” to co-market the product. A close business relationship with local distributors will foster the gain of international knowledge and experience through business, marketing activities, communication and feedback and will if successful, increase the company’s revenues.
A considerable equity mode would be to found a subsidiary under direct control of OCHO New Zealand, however, this would be a much more complex undertaking. Is it worthwhile? How to set up a company in Jakarta? Do we need to employ locals? Do we have the capabilities for successful expatriate programs? The risk and the lack of information makes that option seem to be rather inappropriate at this point of time. A more detailed assessment will follow within that blog.

Fourth; the effect. Going abroad will affect the organization, strategy and structure of the company. Have a look at the I-R Framework. It distinguishes four international strategy types depending on the forces for global integration and local responsiveness that affect the company. Depending on the degree of pressure for local responsiveness, that we will explore at a different point in time, OCHO will either adopt an international or a multi-domestic strategy.

Also considering OCHO’s foreign product diversity and the relative importance of foreign sales over domestic sales the company is likely to adopt an international division structure. For more information about that see Stopford and Wells Model of MNC Organizations.

The change in strategy and structure, as well as the new foreign business activity, will have an impact on OCHO’s internal, domestic and international marketing. Internal marketing is about the perspective of having the internal stakeholders of the company as its own customers. Therefore, internal marketing strategies are inevitable for the success of international expansion plans and must align and support the international marketing efforts. This includes the effective communication of the company’s vision, mission and international strategy to efficiently unlock all internal resources. The strategy must be clearly communicated and implemented throughout all business units of the company. Also, the international division structure will expose HR to new needs and functional demands. Internal marketing must ensure that all structures and processes create maximum value for the internal stakeholders of the firm. 

Also, the company will shift from domestic to international marketing. While the domestic marketing practices might be unaffected, marketing strategies have to be extended and adjusted to the international marketing environment. Have a look here for the key definitions and differences. International marketing is substantially more complex and requires more expertise and resources. On the other hand, if done correctly, it can unlock enormous potential for the company on an international scale. The domestic marketing serves here as a reference point to identify differences and similarities to the international market. This concerns all above-mentioned dimensions and delivers necessary implications for the international marketing efforts.

Everything you need to know about Jakarta

The first thing you do when you need some general facts is exploring the Wikipedia introduction section about the subject of interest. That gives us some valuable key information to form our first impression.

Jakarta is the capital of Indonesia and the economic, cultural and political centre of the country. That is not surprising considering that it is Indonesia’s largest city with more than 10 million people. It’s also relatively small which makes it after Tokyo the second populous urban area in the world. A diversity of different cultures shapes the social landscape of the town. Attractive business opportunities and high living standards attract people from around the world. You can imagine Jakarta alias the “Big Durian” as the New York of Indonesia where the beauty and the ugly form an exciting city that never sleeps.

The “Badan Pusat Statistik” also known as BPS-Statistics Indonesia is the official bureau of statistics in Indonesia. Their most recent publication “DKI Jakarta Province in Figures 2020“ delivers useful insights about the Jakarta region. We will just tap into some key aspects that will help us to comprehend the big picture. This includes insights about geography and climate, political structures, demographic and social aspects as well as prices and information about consumer behaviour.

Geography and Climate

Jakarta is hot and humid with an average annual temperature close to 29 degrees, 74% humidity and 98 rain days last year. Jakarta’s coastline provides direct access to the Java Sea. That sounds good for a nice holiday but is a life-threatening environment for chocolate.

The Government

The city is governed by the National Government of Jakarta City and has a special status and autonomy granted by law meaning that all public policies are determined by the local government. It is a presidential representative democratic republic with a separation of powers regarding execution, legislation and judiciary. On the first glance is that an acceptable political environment we could work with. However, there is more information to be gathered especially about policies towards international trade and foreign companies.

Demography and Social Welfare

Jakarta’s population is of young age with one-third of the population between 25 and 44 years old. High numbers of children fuel the annual population growth rate of 1.19% (2010-2019) and the hunger for chocolate. There are as many women than man and the average household consists of 4 persons. That tells us we have two little chocolate consumers per family.

The official unemployment rate is at 6.22 per cent. The average net income of a formal worker is 467 NZD per month (292 NZD for informal workers). Only 3.47 per cent of the population is considered to be poor. The message is clear; living costs are much lower over there than back in New Zealand. Our chocolate made in New Zealand will be expensive.

The educational level seems solid. The participation rates for primary and secondary education are high. The percentage of literate people is close to a 100 per cent, especially within the younger generations. With a Human Development Index of 80.76 Jakarta scores in the category “very high”. That is better than Mexico but lower than Russia.

This is good news overall. We want to have consumers that have high living conditions, a good income and a sophisticated education. OCHO stands for high quality, fair conditions, sustainability and the spirit of New Zealand. Those values are only appealing to people that understand their meaning and impact.

Prices, Consumer Behavior and International Trade

The inflation rate in Jakarta fluctuates around 0% with a positive tendency. The average person spends around 40% of its disposable income on food and 60% on non-food expenditures. Split up in different categories the data states that on average 45 NZD (19% of total average expenditure per capita) is spent on prepared food and beverages which I assume also accounts for chocolate. Considering that OCHO chocolate is priced at 14 NZD for 95g in New Zealand, I already see a problem. But modern problems require modern solutions!

Jakarta imports goods worth more than 800 million NZD from New Zealand already. While this only 0.5% of total imports it suggests the existence of some logistical infrastructure between New Zealand and Jakarta. 

That should be enough information for now. The big picture is made and looks… okay. Further research and observations will follow during the lifetime of the blog for more insights about the opportunities in Jakarta’s Choco Market.
